\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Captchas have evolved from distorted text images to dynamic, multi-step challenges
designed to resist automation while remaining trivially solvable by humans.
A recent class of video-editing captchas presents users with a short clip and a natural-language
instruction such as ``reverse the video, then flip it horizontally.''
Imagine a user trying to log into a secure system, only to be confronted with a 15-second chaotic video clip and the instruction: ``The video has been horizontally mirrored, then sped up 2x. Undo these transformations.'' A human with basic video editing software can quickly apply a horizontal flip and halve the playback speed, bringing a briefly flashing, faint alphanumeric key (e.g., `K9M2P') into clear, readable view. This seemingly simple test effectively filters out bots while remaining manageable for human problem-solvers.
An automated agent must parse the instruction, execute the correct sequence of video transformations, and extract the now-visible key---a compound task spanning language understanding, procedural execution, and visual perception.

Despite the proliferation of video-capable VLMs~\citep{gpt4o,gemini,qwen2vl},
no benchmark systematically tests this compound capability.
While current video understanding benchmarks such as Video-MME \citep{videomme} and LongVideoBench \citep{longvideobench} assess passive temporal reasoning and question answering, they do not require active, sequential manipulation of the visual stream based on language. Conversely, video generation and editing benchmarks like VBench \citep{vbench} evaluate the perceptual quality of generative models rather than the precise execution of rigid transformational rules necessary for agentic problem-solving. Neither paradigm captures the tight coupling of instruction-following, procedural tool use, and visual extraction required here.
CIPHER fills this gap.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Task formulation.} We define the video-editing captcha solving task
        as a three-stage pipeline: instruction parsing (S1), ffmpeg execution (S2),
        and key extraction (S3), enabling fine-grained per-stage diagnostics.

  \item \textbf{Benchmark.} CIPHER provides 250 procedurally generated samples
        across five difficulty levels (L1--L5), varying key visibility,
        operation count (1--4 ops), and instruction ambiguity (exact to compositional).
        Backgrounds are drawn from Kinetics-400 to ensure visual diversity.

  \item \textbf{Metrics.} We propose \emph{Pipeline-F1}, a weighted combination of
        per-stage scores ($0.25 \cdot \text{S1} + 0.25 \cdot \text{S2} + 0.50 \cdot \text{S3}$),
        alongside strict exact match (EM) and character-level F1.

  \item \textbf{Baselines.} We benchmark OCR-only, an agentic MCP pipeline,
        and frontier VLMs (Claude Opus/Sonnet/Haiku, GPT-5.4),
        revealing that S3 key extraction is near-trivial for VLMs in isolation
        but the full agentic pipeline---parsing, execution, temporal
        key localization---remains a challenging integration problem.
\end{enumerate}
