\subsection{Stage bottleneck analysis}

We decompose failures by stage to identify where the pipeline breaks down.
Figure~\ref{fig:stage_breakdown} shows per-level S1/S2/S3 scores for each model.

\paragraph{S1 (parsing)} saturates quickly across models: regex matching achieves high
parse F1 on L1--L3. L5 compositional instructions require VLM-based parsing.

\paragraph{S2 (execution)} failures are rare on L1--L2 but increase with op count (L4--L5),
primarily due to ffmpeg filter chain errors when ops compose non-trivially
(e.g., \texttt{reverse} requires a separate pass before other filters).

\paragraph{S3 (extraction)} is \emph{not} the binding bottleneck for VLMs.
In isolation (given the correct frame), Opus and Sonnet achieve 100\% EM
across all levels. Haiku drops to 75\% on smaller, randomly-positioned keys
(visibility level 2). OCR baselines ($\sim$25\% EM) struggle with background
text from video credits and scene elements.
The true difficulty lies in \textbf{temporal key localization}: the key appears
only during a 2.5--4.5s window within the video, and agents must identify
the correct frames before extraction.

\subsection{NL ambiguity ablation}

Holding key visibility and op count fixed (L2 vs.\ L3), we isolate the effect of
instruction paraphrase. Parse F1 drops by $\Delta$ on paraphrase vs.\ exact phrasing,
with most errors on speed and rotation ops where synonym coverage is lowest.

\subsection{Background influence}

All backgrounds are drawn from Kinetics-400 (natural track).
As a diagnostic, we rerun OCR-only on a 50-sample clean-background subset (BBB clips).
CharF1 improves by $\Delta$, confirming that natural backgrounds introduce
measurable OCR distraction even at high key visibility.

\subsection{Character-level confusion in the near-miss regime}
\label{sec:confusion}

Under the calibrated extraction condition (Section~\ref{sec:gepa}), Gemini Flash
outputs predictions of the correct key length but makes single-character
substitutions that are \emph{consistent} with the visual geometry of the
rendered glyphs, not random guessing. Representative errors from Round~3
\texttt{prog\_3}:
\[
\texttt{TQ573CYX}\!\to\!\texttt{T0573CYK},\quad
\texttt{D3JMQKWH}\!\to\!\texttt{D3JMOKWH},\quad
\texttt{8ER7C6LN}\!\to\!\texttt{BER7CGLN}.
\]
The dominant substitution classes are
$\{\texttt{Q}\!\leftrightarrow\!\texttt{O}, \texttt{Q}\!\leftrightarrow\!\texttt{0}\}$,
$\{\texttt{8}\!\leftrightarrow\!\texttt{B}\}$, and
$\{\texttt{X}\!\leftrightarrow\!\texttt{K}, \texttt{6}\!\leftrightarrow\!\texttt{G}\}$.
These pairs share identical bounding-box silhouettes once the noise-plus-blur
filter discards interior strokes, suggesting that the binding constraint at this
scale is low-frequency glyph shape rather than instruction understanding.
Invalidated Round~4 (fallback to Claude after Gemini was unreachable) produced
$<$12\% char-accuracy and zero positionally correct characters, illustrating that
outside the near-miss regime the same images elicit qualitatively different
failure modes (fabrication vs.\ misreading).
